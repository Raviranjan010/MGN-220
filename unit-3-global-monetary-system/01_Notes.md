## PART 1: CORE LECTURE NOTES (BASED ON COURSE PPT)

### 1. Global Monetary System

#### Meaning
The **Global Monetary System** refers to the network of rules, institutions, agreements, policies, and financial markets that regulate international payments, currency exchanges, and capital flows across national borders.

```
[Domestic Market A] ◄──► [Global Monetary System (Forex, IMF, Banks)] ◄──► [Domestic Market B]
  (Pays in Currency A)         (Converts at Exchange Rate)          (Receives Currency B)
```

This system acts as the financial infrastructure of the world economy. Without it, international trade would revert to barter, and cross-border investment would be impossible.

#### Importance
1. **Facilitates International Trade**: Allows buyers and sellers from different countries to trade goods and services by converting domestic currencies.
2. **Maintains Exchange Rate Stability**: Helps manage currency volatility through coordinated central bank actions and international agreements.
3. **Promotes Global Economic Cooperation**: Standardizes monetary transaction rules, building trust and cooperation among member states.
4. **Supports International Investment**: Reassures multinational companies that their investments, profits, and dividends can be safely converted and repatriated.
5. **Helps Manage Financial Crises**: Provides emergency funding and institutional support to countries suffering from temporary economic shocks.
6. **Encourages Global Economic Growth**: Provides the financial liquidity needed to grease the wheels of international commerce.

#### Example
When an Indian software company (e.g., Infosys) exports services to a US client, the client pays in US Dollars (USD). Infosys uses the global monetary system to convert those dollars into Indian Rupees (INR) through its local bank to pay domestic employees and suppliers.

### 2. Foreign Exchange Market

#### Meaning
The **Foreign Exchange Market** (also known as the **Forex** or **FX** market) is the global, decentralized, over-the-counter (OTC) financial market where currencies are bought, sold, exchanged, and speculated upon. It is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with daily trading volumes exceeding $7.5 trillion, operating 24 hours a day, five days a week.

#### Functions of the Forex Market
1. **Currency Transfer (Conversion)**: The primary function is to transfer purchasing power from one national currency to another.
2. **Providing Credit**: Facilitates international trade by financing goods in transit (e.g., providing letters of credit or short-term trade loans across currencies).
3. **Hedging (Risk Management)**: Provides financial instruments (forwards, futures, options) that allow businesses to protect themselves against adverse exchange rate fluctuations.

#### Participants in the Forex Market
* **Commercial and Investment Banks**: The main players; they facilitate customer transactions and trade for their own accounts (interbank market).
* **Multinational Corporations (MNCs)**: Use the market to pay for foreign imports, convert export revenues, or invest in foreign subsidiaries.
* **Central Banks**: Intervene to manage their country's exchange rate, maintain foreign reserves, and implement monetary policies.
* **Speculators and Arbitrageurs**: Seek to profit from short-term currency price fluctuations (speculators) or exploit price differences across markets (arbitrageurs).
* **Retail Brokers and Individual Investors**: Participate through online platforms for personal investment, travel, or small-scale trading.

### 3. Exchange Rate

#### Meaning
An **Exchange Rate** is the price of one country's currency expressed in terms of another country's currency. It represents the ratio of exchange between two currencies.

```
Base Currency / Quote Currency = Exchange Rate Value
Example: USD/INR = 83.50 (It costs 83.50 INR to buy 1 USD)
```

#### Determinants of Exchange Rates
In a free-floating exchange rate system, rates are determined by supply and demand, which are influenced by:
1. **Inflation Rates**: A country with low inflation sees its currency appreciate as its purchasing power increases relative to other currencies. High inflation leads to depreciation.
2. **Interest Rates**: Higher interest rates offer lenders in an economy a higher return relative to other countries. This attracts foreign capital, increasing demand and causing the currency to appreciate.
3. **Current Account Deficits (Balance of Payments)**: A country with a current account deficit is spending more on foreign trade than it is earning. It must sell its own currency to buy foreign currency to pay for imports, which depreciates the currency.
4. **Public Debt**: Countries with large public debt are less attractive to foreign investors due to fears of inflation and default, leading to currency depreciation.
5. **Terms of Trade**: The ratio of export prices to import prices. If export prices rise faster than import prices, terms of trade improve, increasing currency demand and appreciation.
6. **Political Stability & Economic Performance**: Investors seek stable countries with strong economic growth, driving up demand for their domestic currencies.

### 4. International Monetary System

#### Meaning
The **International Monetary System (IMS)** is the formal framework of rules, institutions, agreements, and procedures that govern exchange rates, international payments, and capital flows between countries.

```
IMS Framework:
├── Exchange Rate Regimes (Fixed, Floating, Pegged)
├── Global Reserve Assets (Gold, USD, Special Drawing Rights)
├── Regulating Institutions (IMF, World Bank, Central Banks)
└── Payment Clearance Systems (SWIFT, Interbank networks)
```

The IMS ensures that the global financial system remains stable, liquid, and predictable, minimizing the risk of systemic financial collapses.

#### Objectives of the IMS
* **Promote Exchange Rate Stability**: Prevent competitive currency devaluations and excessive volatility.
* **Facilitate International Trade**: Create a frictionless system for currency exchange to maximize global trade.
* **Promote International Monetary Cooperation**: Establish a forum (like the IMF) where countries can discuss and resolve global financial problems.
* **Ensure Balance of Payments Adjustment**: Help countries resolve trade and capital account deficits without resorting to destructive trade barriers.
* **Provide Global Liquidity**: Ensure there are enough international reserve assets (like USD or IMF Special Drawing Rights) to finance global transactions.

### 5. Evolution of International Monetary System

The rules governing exchange rates have evolved through three major historical eras:

#### 1. The Gold Standard System (1870s – 1939)
* **Mechanism**: The value of each country's currency was directly linked to a fixed amount of physical gold. Governments stood ready to convert paper currency into physical gold at the fixed rate.
* **Feature**: Exchange rates between currencies were fixed and highly stable. For example, if the US pegged the dollar at $20.67 per ounce of gold and the UK pegged the pound at £4.25 per ounce, the exchange rate was fixed at $4.86 per pound.
* **Collapse**: Suspended during World War I as governments printed money to finance war expenses, leading to hyperinflation. It collapsed permanently during the Great Depression (1930s) as countries engaged in competitive devaluations to boost exports.

#### 2. The Bretton Woods System (1944 – 1973)
* **Mechanism**: Established at a landmark conference in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design a post-WWII financial architecture. Currencies were pegged to the US Dollar ($), and the USD was pegged to gold at a fixed price of $35 per ounce.
* **Feature**: An **adjustable peg** system. Countries kept their currencies within a 1% margin of their USD peg. If a country faced a "fundamental disequilibrium" in its balance of payments, it could devalue or revalue its currency with IMF approval.
* **Institutions Created**: The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank.
* **Collapse**: In the early 1970s, the US ran massive trade deficits and printed excess dollars to fund the Vietnam War and domestic programs. Foreign nations doubted that the US had enough gold to back all dollars. In 1971, President Nixon suspended the USD's gold convertibility (the "Nixon Shock"), and the system collapsed into floating rates by 1973.

#### 3. The Floating Exchange Rate System (1973 – Present)
* **Mechanism**: Most major industrialized nations let market forces of supply and demand determine exchange rates. Governments and central banks occasionally intervene in the market to smooth out extreme fluctuations (a system known as a **Managed Float** or "dirty float").
* **Feature**: Exchange rates change constantly based on economic performance, inflation, interest rates, and speculative flows.

### 6. International Monetary Fund

#### Meaning & Objectives
Established in 1944 under Bretton Woods, the **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** is an international organization headquartered in Washington, D.C., representing 190 member countries. Its primary mandate is to ensure the stability of the international monetary system.

#### Functions of the IMF
1. **Surveillance (Policy Advice)**: Monitors the economic and financial policies of its 190 member countries and provides regular advice to prevent financial crises.
2. **Financial Assistance (Lender of Last Resort)**: Provides short-to-medium-term loans and bailout packages to member countries experiencing severe balance-of-payments crises (when they run out of foreign reserves to pay for imports).
3. **Technical Assistance and Capacity Development**: Provides training and advice to developing nations on tax policy, banking regulations, central banking, and macroeconomic statistics.
4. **Promoting Exchange Stability**: Encourages member nations to avoid competitive devaluations and maintain orderly exchange arrangements.

---

### 7. World Bank

#### Meaning & Role
The **World Bank Group** is an international financial institution established alongside the IMF in 1944. While the IMF focuses on short-term macroeconomic and currency stability, the World Bank focuses on **long-term economic development and poverty reduction**.

It achieves this by:
* Providing low-interest loans, interest-free credits, and grants to developing countries.
* Financing long-term infrastructure projects (roads, bridges, electricity grids, dams, schools, and hospitals).
* Providing technical expertise and policy advice to improve governance, education, healthcare, and environmental sustainability in developing countries.

#### Example
The World Bank providing $500 million in financial support to a developing country to build clean water supply systems, rural road networks, or national health facilities.

---

### 8. Asian Development Bank

#### Meaning & Role
Established in 1966 and headquartered in Manila, Philippines, the **Asian Development Bank (ADB)** is a regional financial institution dedicated to reducing poverty and promoting sustainable social and economic growth in the Asia-Pacific region.

Like the World Bank, the ADB provides loans, grants, equity investments, and technical assistance to its member countries (currently 68 members, of which 49 are from the region).

#### Core Focus Areas:
* **Infrastructure**: Transportation, energy, and water networks.
* **Environment**: Climate change mitigation and clean energy.
* **Regional Integration**: Promoting trade and cooperation between Asian neighbors.
* **Education and Health**: Upgrading schools, vocational training, and healthcare facilities.

#### Example
The ADB providing a loan to an Asian country (like Bangladesh or Vietnam) to build high-speed urban transit lines, install solar power grids, or upgrade agricultural supply chains.

### 9. Case Study: IMF Support During the Asian Financial Crisis (1997)

#### Background
In the 1990s, East Asian economies (the "Asian Tigers" like Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, and South Korea) experienced rapid growth funded by massive inflows of foreign capital. However, much of this debt was short-term, denominated in USD, and held by private banks and companies. 

The crisis triggered when Thailand's central bank could no longer defend the Thai Baht's peg to the USD due to declining exports and speculative attacks. In July 1997, Thailand was forced to float the Baht, which immediately collapsed in value.

```
Hot Capital Inflows ──► Excessive USD Debt ──► Peg Collapses ──► Currency Panic ──► Regional Contagion
```

#### Contagion & Impact
Panic spread like wildfire. Foreign investors withdrew capital from the entire region (contagion). The currencies of Indonesia, South Korea, and the Philippines crashed. Private companies with USD debt faced bankruptcy because they now needed far more domestic currency to repay their loans. Banks collapsed, unemployment soared, and trade ground to a halt.

#### IMF Intervention & Reforms
To stabilize the region, the IMF arranged a massive $110 billion bailout package for Thailand, Indonesia, and South Korea. However, the funds came with strict **conditionalities**:
* **High Interest Rates**: Central banks had to raise interest rates to defend their currencies and prevent capital flight.
* **Fiscal Austerity**: Governments had to cut spending and raise taxes to balance their budgets.
* **Structural Reforms**: Countries had to close bankrupt banks, break up family-owned conglomerates (like South Korea's *chaebols*), and open domestic markets to foreign ownership.

#### Results & Lessons
The IMF packages eventually stabilized the currencies, restored investor confidence, and helped the region recover by 1999. However, the harsh austerity measures caused widespread unemployment and corporate bankruptcies, leading to severe public criticism of the IMF. 

The primary lesson was the danger of relying on short-term foreign debt and the importance of maintaining robust, well-regulated domestic banking systems.

### 10. Currency Exposures

When a firm operates internationally, it faces three distinct types of foreign exchange exposure:

```
                          TYPES OF FOREX EXPOSURE
        ┌─────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┐
        ▼                         ▼                         ▼
Transaction Exposure      Translation Exposure      Economic Exposure
(Short-term cash flow)     (Accounting paper risk)   (Long-term competitiveness)
```

#### 1. Transaction Exposure
* **Definition**: The risk that the actual cash value of a contract will change due to exchange rate movements before the transaction is settled.
* **Nature**: Affects outstanding invoices, receivables, and payables. It is felt directly in the short-term cash flows of the company.
* **Example**: A US company imports German machine parts for €100,000 on 3-month credit. If the Euro appreciates against the USD during those 3 months, the US company will have to spend more USD than expected to buy the €100,000.

#### 2. Translation (Accounting) Exposure
* **Definition**: The risk that exchange rate fluctuations will distort the consolidated financial statements of a multinational company when translating foreign subsidiaries' balance sheets and income statements into the parent company's reporting currency.
* **Nature**: An accounting paper gain or loss; it does not affect actual cash flows unless the foreign subsidiary is sold.
* **Example**: A Japanese subsidiary of a US firm holds assets worth 100 million Yen. If the Yen depreciates, the parent firm will report a lower dollar asset value on its consolidated annual balance sheet.

#### 3. Economic (Operating) Exposure
* **Definition**: The risk that unexpected exchange rate changes will permanently impact a firm's future cash flows, international competitiveness, and long-term market value.
* **Nature**: Long-term and strategic. It affects pricing power, supply costs, and market share.
* **Example**: A persistent appreciation of the Japanese Yen makes Toyota's exports to the US more expensive than US-built cars, permanently reducing Toyota's market share and future sales volume.

### 11. Hedging Instruments

Multinational corporations use four primary derivatives to hedge (protect) against currency risk:

#### 1. Forward Contracts
* **Description**: A customized, legally binding agreement between a company and a bank to exchange a specific amount of currency at a predetermined exchange rate (the Forward Rate) on a specific future date.
* **Market**: Traded Over-the-Counter (OTC) (not on public exchanges).
* **Pros**: Highly customizable to any exact amount or delivery date; free to set up (implicit cost in the spread).
* **Cons**: No flexibility (must be executed); carries counterparty credit risk (the bank or firm might default).

#### 2. Futures Contracts
* **Description**: Standardized contracts to exchange a set amount of currency at a specified price on fixed delivery dates.
* **Market**: Traded on public exchanges (e.g., Chicago Mercantile Exchange - CME).
* **Pros**: No counterparty default risk (guaranteed by the exchange clearinghouse); highly liquid and easy to trade.
* **Cons**: Rigid (standardized sizes and maturity dates like March, June, September); requires maintaining a margin account with daily cash deposits.

#### 3. Currency Options
* **Description**: A contract that gives the buyer the **right, but not the obligation**, to buy (Call Option) or sell (Put Option) a currency at a set rate (Strike Price) on or before a specific maturity date.
* **Cost**: The buyer must pay an upfront, non-refundable fee called a **Premium** to the seller.
* **Pros**: Maximum flexibility. If the market rate moves favorably, the buyer can let the option expire and buy/sell at the better spot rate, capturing the upside.
* **Cons**: High upfront cost (the premium must be paid regardless of whether the option is used).

#### 4. Currency Swaps
* **Description**: A transaction where two parties exchange principal and interest payments in different currencies. After a set period, they swap back the principal at a predetermined rate.
* **Usage**: Often used by MNCs to secure cheaper debt in foreign currencies where they lack local brand recognition.
* **Example**: A US company wants to borrow Euros, and a German company wants to borrow USD. They borrow in their domestic markets (where interest rates are lowest for them) and swap the currencies, saving on interest costs.

### 12. Currency Arbitrage & Speculation

#### Currency Speculation
* **Mechanism**: Speculators deliberately take on foreign exchange risk by purchasing currencies they believe will appreciate or selling currencies they expect to depreciate. Unlike hedgers who seek to eliminate risk, speculators welcome risk in search of high returns.
* **Impact**: Speculation can stabilize markets by providing liquidity, but extreme speculative attacks can trigger currency crashes (as seen in Thailand in 1997).

#### Currency Arbitrage
Arbitrage is the risk-free purchase of a currency in one market for immediate resale in another market to profit from temporary price discrepancies.
* **Two-Point Arbitrage**: Capitalizing on rate differences between two geographical markets (e.g., buying Euros in London at $1.08 and selling them immediately in New York at $1.09).
* **Three-Point (Triangular) Arbitrage**: Exploiting price discrepancies between three different currencies. 

```
Step 1: Convert USD to EUR ──► Step 2: Convert EUR to GBP ──► Step 3: Convert GBP back to USD (for profit)
```

*Note:* Because of high-frequency trading algorithms, arbitrage opportunities exist for only milliseconds before supply and demand force the prices back into equilibrium.

### 13. Advanced Exchange Rate Determination Theories

To explain and predict long-term exchange rate movements, economists use two primary theories:

#### 1. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)
* **Core Idea**: The Law of One Price. It states that in the absence of transaction costs and trade barriers, identical goods must sell for the same price in different countries when expressed in a common currency.
* **Formula**:
  $$\text{Exchange Rate (Currency A / Currency B)} = \frac{\text{Price of Good in Country A}}{\text{Price of Good in Country B}}$$
* **Application (The Big Mac Index)**: Developed by *The Economist*, it compares the price of a McDonald's Big Mac burger worldwide to check if a currency is overvalued or undervalued relative to the USD. If a Big Mac costs $5.50 in the US and 200 INR in India, the PPP exchange rate should be $200 / 5.50 = 36.36 \text{ INR/USD}$. If the actual exchange rate is 83 INR/USD, the Indian Rupee is significantly undervalued.
* **Limitation**: Ignores transport costs, government tariffs, and the fact that many services (like haircuts) cannot be traded across borders.

#### 2. Interest Rate Parity (IRP)
* **Core Idea**: IRP states that the difference in interest rates between two countries must equal the difference between the spot exchange rate and the forward exchange rate.
* **Mechanism**: If one country offers higher interest rates, its currency must depreciate in the forward market by an equal amount so that global investors cannot earn a risk-free arbitrage profit by moving funds between countries (Covered Interest Arbitrage).
* **Implication**: High interest rates prevent capital flight only if investors expect the currency to remain stable or if they can lock in a favorable forward contract rate.

### 14. Criticisms of International Financial Institutions

While the IMF, World Bank, and ADB promote development, they face significant criticisms:

#### 1. IMF Conditionality and Austerity
* **Criticism**: The IMF's Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) demand harsh austerity measures (cutting public wages, raising VAT, removing food/fuel subsidies).
* **Impact**: These measures reduce government deficits but often trigger recessions, worsen poverty, and cause social unrest. Critics argue the IMF prioritizes repaying foreign banks over the welfare of local citizens.

#### 2. Voting Power Imbalance
* **Criticism**: Voting power in the IMF and World Bank is determined by financial quotas (how much money a country contributes).
* **Impact**: The United States holds over 16% of the voting share in the IMF, giving it a unilateral veto over major structural changes (which require an 85% majority). Developing nations in Africa and Asia have minimal voting power despite being the primary recipients of loans. By unwritten agreement, the head of the IMF is always a European, and the head of the World Bank is always an American.

#### 3. Environmental and Social Displacements
* **Criticism**: World Bank and ADB megaprojects (like large dams, coal plants, and highway systems) have historically displaced indigenous populations and caused severe environmental degradation.

### 15. Solved Sovereign & Corporate Case Studies

#### Sovereign Case: Sri Lanka's 2022 Economic Collapse & IMF Bailout
* **Background**: In 2022, Sri Lanka experienced a severe foreign reserve crisis due to tax cuts, a ban on chemical fertilizers (ruining agriculture), and a collapse in tourism. The country ran out of USD to import fuel, medicine, and food. The Sri Lankan Rupee (LKR) depreciated by over 45%, and the government defaulted on its $51 billion foreign debt.
* **The Bailout**: The IMF approved a $3 billion loan under the Extended Fund Facility (EFF) in 2023.
* **The Austerity Reality**: To secure the loan, Sri Lanka had to:
  * Double personal income taxes.
  * Increase electricity tariffs by over 66% to reduce state utility losses.
  * Remove fuel subsidies, causing transport costs to skyrocket.
* **Exam Analysis**: This case demonstrates the IMF's role as the **lender of last resort** and highlights why IMF conditionalities are controversial. It shows that sovereign currency stability requires strict domestic fiscal reforms.

#### Corporate Case: Porsche's Forex Hedging Strategy
* **Background**: Porsche manufactures the vast majority of its sports cars in Germany (costs in Euros) but sells nearly half of its production in North America (revenues in USD). If the USD depreciates against the Euro, Porsche's profit margins are wiped out.
* **The Strategy**: Unlike other automakers, Porsche hedge-rolled 100% of its estimated USD revenues up to 3 years in advance using **Currency Put Options**. This guaranteed that Porsche could sell its USD revenues at a pre-set rate of, for example, €0.90 per dollar.
* **The Outcome**: In the mid-2000s, the USD fell to record lows against the Euro. Competitors like BMW and Mercedes suffered massive losses on US exports. Porsche, however, posted record profits because it exercised its options to convert USD at its favorable pre-set rate. This case shows how strategic hedging can be a source of competitive advantage.
