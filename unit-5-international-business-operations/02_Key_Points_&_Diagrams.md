## 🔑 SECTION 1: KEY POINTS — CORE CURRICULUM

### ▶ Export/Import & Documentation
*   **Bill of Lading (B/L)**: Issued by the carrier to the exporter. Acts as cargo receipt, transport contract, and document of title (proves ownership).
*   **Commercial Invoice**: The seller's bill to the buyer detailing goods descriptions, prices, and Incoterms.
*   **Letter of Credit (L/C)**: A bank-issued guarantee promising to pay the exporter once correct shipping documents are presented. Resolves the global buyer-seller trust deficit.
*   **Countertrade**: Cashless trade agreements where payment is made in goods/services. Used during forex currency shortages (5 types: Barter, Counterpurchase, Offset, Buyback, Switch Trading).

### ▶ Global Production & Outsourcing
*   **Global Production**: Manufacturing components and assembling goods across multiple countries to capture cost and location economies.
*   **Location Factors**:
    *   *Country Factors*: Wages, land costs, taxes, and infrastructure.
    *   *Technological Factors*: Setup fixed costs (high setup = centralized plant) and flexible manufacturing (lean production).
    *   *Product Factors*: Value-to-weight ratio (high ratio = centralized + air cargo; low ratio = regional plants + ocean cargo).
*   **Make-or-Buy Decision**:
    *   *Make (In-house)*: Protects proprietary technology and controls quality and schedules (e.g., Apple designing its own chips).
    *   *Buy (Outsource)*: Provides strategic flexibility and avoids high fixed setup capital (e.g., Apple outsourcing phone assembly to Foxconn).

### ▶ International Logistics
*   **Logistics**: Planning and controlling the physical movement and storage of goods across borders.
*   **Transportation Modes**:
    *   *Ocean Freight*: Cheap, handles bulk, slow (handles 90% of global trade).
    *   *Air Freight*: Fast, secure, highly expensive (used for high value-to-weight items).
*   **Logistics Components**: Transportation, warehousing, inventory management (JIT), packaging, and customs clearance.

### 💡 Memory Hook: Bill of Lading Functions — "R C T"
*   **R** — **R**eceipt (Proof carrier received the goods)
*   **C** — **C**ontract (Terms of transport)
*   **T** — **T**itle (Document of legal ownership)

### 💡 Make vs. Buy Decision Criteria Cheat Card
*   Choose **MAKE** if: High IP sensitivity, high asset specificity, high quality focus.
*   Choose **BUY** if: High cost pressure, need for flexibility, low setup capital.

## 🎯 EXAM TIPS FOR 100% MARKS

### ✅ How to Draft Letter of Credit Descriptions
1.  **State the Core Problem**: Explain the **Trust Deficit** (seller fears non-payment; buyer fears non-shipment).
2.  **Introduce the Intermediary**: Explain that the L/C shifts credit risk from the buyer to the buyer's bank (**Issuing Bank**).
3.  **Use the Sequence Diagram**: Draw the 10-step sequence flow showing the path of the contract, L/C, goods, Bill of Lading, and payments.
4.  **Emphasize Document Focus**: Bold the fact that **"Banks deal in documents, not goods."** Exporters get paid based on correct documents, not product delivery.

### ✅ Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid
*   ❌ **Confusing Bill of Lading and L/C**: Bill of Lading is a shipping ownership document issued by the carrier. L/C is a financial payment guarantee issued by the bank.
*   ❌ **Offset vs. Counterpurchase Confusion**: Under counterpurchase, the firm must buy from the *same* customer entity or specific firm. Under offset, the firm can buy components from *any* local firm in the host nation (used in defense).
*   ❌ **Outsourcing Core Technology**: Never recommend outsourcing a firm's core intellectual property (e.g., do not outsource proprietary microchip design; outsource standard assembly).
*   ❌ **Ignoring Value-to-Weight Ratio**: When explaining transportation modes, always cite the value-to-weight ratio. High value/low weight = Air (iPhone). Low value/high weight = Ocean (Cement).
