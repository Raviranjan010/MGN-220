## PART 1: CORE LECTURE NOTES (BASED ON COURSE PPT)

### 1. Global Marketing

#### Meaning
**Global Marketing** is the process of planning, pricing, promoting, and distributing goods, services, and ideas in international markets. It involves identifying customer needs across different countries and tailoring marketing strategies to satisfy those needs while building a consistent global brand identity.

#### Scope
It ranges from standardizing marketing campaigns globally to adapting them for specific cultural, economic, and legal differences in host countries.

---

### 2. Types of Global Marketing Strategies

MNCs choose between two primary marketing approaches:
* **Standardization**: Selling the exact same product, using the same brand image, advertisements, and distribution channels across all global markets.
  * *Example*: Apple positioning and selling the iPhone identically worldwide.
* **Adaptation (Localization)**: Modifying the marketing mix (Product, Price, Place, Promotion) to match the cultural, economic, and legal characteristics of each host country.
  * *Example*: McDonald's modifying its menu in India to include vegetarian and chicken-based options.

---

### 3. Advantages of Global Marketing

According to the course PPT, successful global marketing provides:
1. **Access to Global Markets**: Allows companies to expand their customer base beyond domestic borders.
2. **Brand Recognition**: Builds a unified international image, increasing customer trust.
3. **Economies of Scale**: Standardized advertising and packaging reduce production and design costs.
4. **Improved Revenues**: Drives higher sales volume by targeting diverse country segments.

---

### 4. Challenges of Global Marketing

* **Cultural Differences**: Consumer tastes, religious values, and food habits vary heavily (e.g., beef restrictions in India).
* **Legal & Regulatory Barriers**: Countries have different laws regarding advertising, product labeling, and product safety.
* **Language Barriers**: Misinterpreting slogans or brand names can alienate local consumers.
* **Fierce Competition**: Competing against entrenched domestic brands who understand local preferences.
* **Economic Disparities**: Varied purchasing power requires different pricing strategies.

---

### 5. Research & Development (R&D)

#### Meaning
**Research and Development (R&D)** refers to the investigative activities a firm undertakes to innovate, design, and introduce new products and technologies, or improve existing ones.

#### Importance in Global Markets
* Helps companies stay competitive in fast-moving industries (like electronics or pharmaceuticals).
* Enables firms to adapt products to meet specific regulatory or consumer needs of foreign markets.
* Drives value creation by introducing advanced technologies.

#### Example
Global pharmaceutical companies investing billions in R&D to discover and test new drugs that can be patented and sold worldwide.

---

### 6. Challenges of Global R&D

* **High Cost & Uncertainty**: R&D requires massive investments with no guarantee of commercial success.
* **Intellectual Property (IP) Risks**: Risk of technology copying in countries with weak patent laws.
* **Coordination Difficulties**: Managing decentralized R&D teams across different time zones and cultures.
* **Mismatch with Market Needs**: Designing technologies that are too expensive for consumers or fail to match local market demands.

---

### 7. Global Human Resource Management (GHRM)

#### Meaning
**Global Human Resource Management (GHRM)** is the process of recruiting, selecting, training, compensating, and evaluating employees in multinational corporations operating across different countries.

#### Scope
It focuses on managing a culturally diverse workforce, handling international employee assignments (expatriates), and coordinating HR practices between corporate headquarters and foreign subsidiaries.

---

### 8. Core Functions of GHRM

1. **Recruitment & Selection**: Hiring qualified professionals globally (e.g., sourcing IT engineers from India).
2. **Training & Development**: Preparing employees for global roles and offering cross-cultural training.
3. **Compensation Management**: Structuring salaries, taxes, and benefits to match local living standards and laws.
4. **Performance Management**: Standardizing employee evaluations across different regional offices.
5. **Employee Relations**: Managing grievance procedures, unions, and labor relations under host-country laws.

---

### 9. Advantages & Challenges of GHRM

#### Advantages
* **Global Talent Pool**: Accesses the best skills worldwide.
* **Enhanced Productivity**: Structured training and fair compensation drive employee performance.
* **Better Cultural Understanding**: Diverse teams foster creative problem-solving and innovation.
* **Supports Subsidiary Expansion**: Hiring local managers ensures smoother host-country operations.

#### Challenges
* **Cultural Conflicts**: Managing different work habits and communication styles.
* **Relocation Hurdles**: Handling immigration visas and expat family moves.
* **Diverse Labor Laws**: Complying with varying local union, safety, and wage regulations.
* **Expatriate Failure**: High cost of managers returning early from foreign assignments.

---

### 10. Case Study: Samsung's Global Business Strategy

#### Background
Samsung Corporation (South Korea) is a global electronics giant operating in over 80 countries, selling smartphones, TVs, and home appliances. Its success relies on an integrated model:

#### Global Marketing Integration
Samsung uses a hybrid marketing approach:
* **Standardization**: It maintains a premium, unified brand image for flagship products (e.g., Galaxy S series).
* **Adaptation**: It customizes product lines and pricing by country. For instance, it sells high-end foldables in developed markets, while offering budget-friendly, local-feature models in developing countries like India.

#### Global R&D Network
Samsung invests billions in R&D, establishing research centers globally:
* Software design centers in Silicon Valley and India.
* Hardware R&D in South Korea.
* Design studios in Europe.
This decentralized R&D network ensures Samsung stays at the cutting edge of tech (foldable screens, AI) while understanding local consumer needs.

#### Global Human Resource Management
Samsung manages a highly diverse workforce. It recruits local engineering talent globally, runs cross-cultural training programs, and establishes leadership rotations. High performers are moved across global units to build a collaborative, innovative corporate culture.

### 11. The International Marketing Mix (4Ps) Deep Dive

MNCs must systematically adapt or standardize each of the 4Ps:

#### 1. Product
* **Cultural & Religious Constraints**: Ingredients must respect local norms (e.g., Halal certification, vegetarian menus).
* **Economic Adaptations**: Selling smaller packaging units (sachets) in developing nations (e.g., shampoo sachets in India) to match lower daily purchasing power.

#### 2. Price
* **Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices in different countries based on demand elasticity (e.g., charging higher prices for textbooks in the US and lower prices in India).
* **Price Escalation**: The inevitable increase in product price in foreign markets due to shipping costs, customs tariffs, and local distributor markups.
* **Dumping**: Selling products below production cost in foreign markets to destroy local competition (prohibited under WTO rules).

#### 3. Place (Distribution Channels)
* **Retail Concentration**:
  * *Concentrated Retail*: A few large superstores dominate (e.g., Walmart in the US). MNCs deal directly with a few powerful buyers.
  * *Fragmented Retail*: Thousands of tiny mom-and-pop shops dominate (e.g., Kirana stores in India). Requires long distribution channels with multiple wholesalers.
* **Channel Length**: The number of intermediaries between the producer and the final consumer. Fragmented retail leads to long channel lengths.

#### 4. Promotion (Communication)
* **Standardized Ads**: Consistent brand message, lower costs, but risks cultural misalignment.
* **Adapted Ads**: Custom local slogans and actors (e.g., translating slogans carefully to avoid embarrassing literal translations).

---

### 12. Global R&D Coordination & Cross-Functional Integration

To succeed, global R&D must be tightly integrated with **Marketing** and **Production**:
* Mismatch with Production leads to designing high-tech products that are too complex or expensive to manufacture in high volume.
* Mismatch with Marketing leads to designing products that fail to satisfy customer needs.
* *Solution:* Cross-functional teams containing members from R&D, Marketing, and Manufacturing located in key regional hubs.

### 13. Staffing Policies in MNCs: The EPG Framework

Developed by Howard Perlmutter, the EPG framework identifies three staffing orientations:

```
                            EPG STAFFING SPECTRUM
        ┌─────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────┐
        ▼                             ▼                             ▼
Ethnocentric Staffing         Polycentric Staffing          Geocentric Staffing
• Filled by: PCNs (HQ staff)  • Filled by: HCNs (Locals)    • Filled by: Best candidate
• Advantage: Protects culture • Advantage: High local fit   • Advantage: Global talent cadre
• Disadvantage: Myopia, bias  • Disadvantage: HQ-Sub gap    • Disadvantage: High cost, visas
```

#### A. Ethnocentric Staffing
Key management positions in foreign subsidiaries are held by Parent-Country Nationals (PCNs).
* *Strategic Fit:* International Strategy.
* *Why:* To transfer core competencies and maintain strict corporate culture control.
* *Drawback:* Cultural myopia (inability to understand host culture) and local staff resentment.

#### B. Polycentric Staffing
Host-Country Nationals (HCNs) manage the local subsidiaries, while PCNs run corporate headquarters.
* *Strategic Fit:* Localization Strategy.
* *Why:* Local managers naturally understand the local language, laws, and culture.
* *Drawback:* Isolation between headquarters and subsidiaries; limits host-country staff from reaching top HQ roles.

#### C. Geocentric Staffing
The best people are sought for key jobs throughout the MNC, regardless of their nationality.
* *Strategic Fit:* Transnational Strategy.
* *Why:* Builds a globally minded executive cadre.
* *Drawback:* High relocation expenses, global salary alignment costs, and visa/immigration barriers.

### 14. Expatriate Management

#### Expatriate Failure
An **Expatriate (Expat)** is a PCN or TCN sent to work in a foreign subsidiary. Expatriate failure is the premature return of an expat manager to their home country due to poor performance.

#### Primary Causes of Expatriate Failure (Tung's Study)
1. **Spouse/Family Inability to Adjust (Ranked #1)**: The family struggles with culture shock, isolation, schools, or climate.
2. **Manager's Personal Inability to Adapt**: Stress, lack of cultural sensitivity, or language barriers.
3. **Lack of Emotional Maturity**: Inability to relate to local employees.
4. **Overwhelming Job Scale**: Coping with complex overseas responsibilities without a home network.

#### Expatriate Training (Three Tiers)
To minimize failure, pre-departure training must include:
* **Cultural Training**: Educating the expat and family on the host country's history, values, and workplace norms.
* **Language Training**: Basic conversational fluency to help the family integrate locally.
* **Practical Training**: Assisting with housing, local transport, banking, and school enrollments.

---

### 15. Repatriation

**Repatriation** is the process of bringing an expatriate manager back to their home country operations. 

#### The Reintegration Challenge
Many MNCs fail to plan for repatriation. Expat managers often return to find:
* Their international experience is ignored or undervalued.
* They are placed in lower-profile or redundant roles.
* Their previous positions are occupied, leading to frustration and high turnover (many leave within a year of return).
* *Solution:* Mentorship programs, career path planning before departure, and placing returned expats in roles that leverage their global experience.

### 16. Solved Corporate Case Studies

#### Case 1: McDonald's Glocal Marketing Mix in India
* **Background**: McDonald's could not sell beef (Hindu restrictions) or pork (Muslim restrictions) in India.
* **The Glocal Adaptations**:
  * **Product**: Eliminated beef/pork. Developed chicken-based (Maharaja Mac) and potato-based (McAloo Tikki) menus. Strict segregation of vegetarian and non-vegetarian kitchens.
  * **Price**: Developed the "Happy Price Menu" (starting at 20-30 INR) to attract middle-class students.
  * **Place**: Built local cold storage supply chains to source fresh lettuce and potatoes locally, reducing costs.
  * **Promotion**: Changed advertising campaigns to focus on local family festivals and emotions.
* **Takeaway**: Illustrates how adapting the 4Ps is essential to survive cultural taboos.

#### Case 2: Sony's Geocentric GHRM Strategy
* **Background**: Sony Corporation historically staffed key US and European divisions with Japanese PCNs, causing local managers to feel capped by a "bamboo ceiling."
* **The Reforms**: Under CEO Howard Stringer (first non-Japanese CEO), Sony set up global talent review boards. It identified top-performing local managers in the US and Europe and placed them in top roles in Tokyo, rotating talent globally.
* **Takeaway**: Geocentric staffing removes nationalistic biases and builds a collaborative, transnational management team.
