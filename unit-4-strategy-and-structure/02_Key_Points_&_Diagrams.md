## 🔑 SECTION 1: KEY POINTS — CORE CURRICULUM

### ▶ Strategy in International Business
*   **Strategy**: Actions taken by managers to maximize firm value (profitability and revenue growth).
*   **Cost Pressures**: Push firms to standardize products to capture economies of scale. High in commodity markets.
*   **Local Responsiveness Pressures**: Push firms to customize products for host-country tastes, rules, and distribution channels.
*   **GLIT Matrix**: Maps cost pressures vs. local responsiveness pressures:
    *   **Global Standardization**: High cost / Low local pressure. Standardized products (e.g., Intel).
    *   **Localization**: Low cost / High local pressure. Customized products (e.g., Heinz).
    *   **International**: Low cost / Low local pressure. Transferring home-competencies (e.g., Rolex).
    *   **Transnational**: High cost / High local pressure. Dual balance (e.g., Caterpillar).

### ▶ Foreign Entry Strategies
*   **Exporting**: Simple, low financial risk, high transport costs, exposed to tariffs.
*   **Turnkey Projects**: Builder sets up plant, trains staff, and hands over keys. Great for specialized engineering.
*   **Licensing**: Licensor grants IP rights to licensee for royalties. Low setup cost; risk of losing IP control.
*   **Franchising**: Licensing in service sector with strict operational rules (e.g., McDonald's).
*   **Joint Venture (JV)**: Shared investment, risk, and control with a local partner (e.g., Tata Starbucks).
*   **Wholly Owned Subsidiary (WOS)**: 100% ownership via Greenfield (build from scratch) or Acquisition (buy firm). Maximum control, cost, and risk.

### ▶ Organizational Structure & Controls
*   **Centralization**: Concentrates decision power at HQ. High coordination; slow response.
*   **Decentralization**: Distributes decision power to subsidiary managers. Fast local response; poor coordination.
*   **Worldwide Area Structure**: Divided by geography. Supports Localization strategy.
*   **Worldwide Product Division Structure**: Divided by product lines. Supports Global Standardization strategy.
*   **Global Matrix Structure**: Dual reporting lines. Supports Transnational strategy. Highly complex.
*   **Control Systems**: Output controls (numerical targets), Bureaucratic controls (budgets/rules), Cultural controls (corporate values).

## 🎯 EXAM TIPS FOR 100% MARKS

### ✅ How to Tackle Entry Mode Case Questions
1.  **Identify Core Technology Sensitivity**: If the firm has highly sensitive proprietary technology, recommend **Wholly Owned Subsidiary (Greenfield)** to prevent IP leaks (apply the Tesla Shanghai case).
2.  **Analyze Capital and Risk Constraints**: If the firm lacks funds or wants to expand quickly, recommend **Franchising** (for services, e.g., Subway) or **Licensing** (for manufacturing).
3.  **Evaluate Host-Country Regulations**: If the host nation legally restricts foreign ownership, recommend **Joint Ventures** (apply the Starbucks-Tata India case).
4.  **Consider Speed to Market**: If time-to-market is critical and competitors are aggressive, recommend **Acquisition** (apply the Lenovo-IBM PC case).

### ✅ Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid
*   ❌ **Confusing Licensing and Franchising**: Remember: licensing is primarily for manufacturing/patents (e.g., pharmaceuticals). Franchising is for service brands (e.g., fast food) and involves much stricter operational rules.
*   ❌ **Greenfield vs. Acquisition Setup**: Do not say Greenfield is faster than Acquisition. Greenfield is built from scratch and is slow; Acquisition purchases an active company and is fast.
*   ❌ **Worldwide Area Structure vs. Product Division**: Do not confuse their strategic alignments. Worldwide Area = Localization strategy (high adaptation). Worldwide Product Division = Global Standardization strategy (standardized).
*   ❌ **Failing to Draw the Matrix**: If asked about the four strategic choices, always draw the 2x2 Integration-Responsiveness Matrix (GLIT Grid) to earn bonus presentation marks.
