## PART 1: CORE LECTURE NOTES (BASED ON COURSE PPT)

### 1. Strategy of International Business

#### Meaning
An **International Business Strategy** is the set of long-term plans and actions adopted by multinational corporations (MNCs) to coordinate operations, compete successfully in foreign markets, and maximize firm value (profitability and revenue growth). It guides key decisions such as market entry, production locations, product pricing, marketing campaigns, and global resource allocation.

#### Core Objectives of Global Strategy
* **Expand into Global Markets**: Access new consumer bases to drive sales growth.
* **Increase Global Market Share**: Build market dominance over international competitors.
* **Achieve Competitive Advantage**: Offer superior products, lower prices, or better customer services.
* **Reduce Production and Operating Costs**: Relocate manufacturing to lower-cost nations.
* **Access Global Resources and Technology**: Secure raw materials, specialized skill pools, and advanced technologies.
* **Increase Profitability and Business Growth**: Drive economies of scale and utilize location economies.

---

### 2. Importance of International Business Strategy

According to the course PPT, developing a structured international strategy is vital because:
1. **Helps Companies Compete in Global Markets**: Enables firms to analyze foreign competitors and build unique capabilities to beat them.
2. **Expands Market Opportunities**: Opens doors to new customer segments in various geographical regions.
3. **Improves Business Growth and Profitability**: Drives higher margins by expanding the sales base.
4. **Helps Utilize Global Resources Efficiently**: Maximizes efficiency by combining inputs from different countries.
5. **Reduces Business Risk Through Market Diversification**: Minimizes vulnerability to localized economic downturns; poor sales in one country can be offset by strong performance in another.
6. **Encourages Innovation and Knowledge Sharing**: Exposes firms to diverse global ideas, technologies, and management practices.

---

### 3. Entry Strategy

#### Meaning
An **Entry Strategy** is the specific approach or business model chosen by a company to introduce its goods, services, and operations into a foreign market. 

```
   Low Investment / Low Risk / Low Control ─────────────► High Investment / High Risk / High Control
                 (Exporting)                                 (Wholly Owned Subsidiary)
```

#### Importance of Selection
Choosing the right entry strategy is a high-stakes decision because it determines:
* The level of financial investment and capital required.
* The amount of operational control the firm retains over marketing, manufacturing, and IP.
* The level of country and economic risk the firm is exposed to.
* The degree of organizational resources and staff commitment needed.

---

### 4. Types of Foreign Entry Strategies

MNCs select from five primary modes of entry depending on their capabilities and market conditions:
1. **Exporting**: Selling domestic-made goods to customers in another country.
2. **Licensing**: Allowing a foreign firm to use proprietary IP in exchange for a fee.
3. **Franchising**: Selling a service brand and business system in exchange for royalties.
4. **Joint Venture (JV)**: Forming a new, shared business entity with a local partner.
5. **Wholly Owned Subsidiary (WOS)**: Establishing 100% ownership of foreign operations.

---

### 5. Exporting

#### Meaning
The simplest and most common entry mode. Goods are manufactured in the home country and shipped to foreign markets through local distributors, trade agents, or direct channels.

#### Advantages
* **Low Setup Cost**: No need to build expensive manufacturing plants in the host country.
* **Low Risk**: If the market fails, the firm has no foreign factories or physical assets to write off.
* **Location Economies**: Allows the firm to manufacture in a single central location to gain economies of scale.

#### Disadvantages
* **High Transport Costs**: Shipping bulky goods long distances can destroy margins.
* **Tariffs and Trade Barriers**: Host governments can impose high import duties.
* **Distributor Conflicts**: Relying on local agents can lead to poor marketing or service control.

#### Example
Indian textile and apparel firms manufacturing products in Gujarat or Tamil Nadu and exporting them directly to retail chains in Europe and the US.

---

### 6. Licensing

#### Meaning
A contract where a firm (the licensor) grants the rights to use intangible property (patents, technology, designs, trademarks) to a foreign entity (the licensee) for a specified period in exchange for a royalty fee.

#### Advantages
* **Minimal Financial Risk**: The licensee provides all the capital to set up production.
* **Bypasses Trade Barriers**: Since production is local to the host country, the firm avoids import tariffs and transport costs.
* **Leverages Local Knowledge**: The licensee handles local hiring, marketing, and distribution.

#### Disadvantages
* **Loss of Intellectual Property (IP) Control**: The licensee might copy the technology and become a competitor once the contract ends.
* **No Tight Coordination**: The licensor cannot use profits from one country to subsidize expansion in another.
* **Quality Risks**: The licensee might fail to maintain product quality, damaging the brand.

#### Example
A US pharmaceutical company licensing its patented drug formula to an Indian drug manufacturer to produce and distribute the medicine locally.

---

### 7. Franchising

#### Meaning
A specialized form of licensing used primarily in the service sector. The franchisor sells its brand name, business model, and operational guidelines, but insists that the franchisee run the business under strict rules.

#### Advantages
* **Rapid Global Expansion**: Franchises are funded by local owners, enabling fast expansion without high capital.
* **Low Cost and Risk**: Host-country franchisees absorb all operational setup costs.
* **High Motivation**: Local owners manage day-to-day operations, ensuring high dedication.

#### Disadvantages
* **Quality Control Difficulties**: Monitoring hundreds of global franchisees is highly complex.
* **Brand Reputation Risk**: A single franchisee serving poor food or offering bad service can damage the entire global brand.
* **Profit Sharing**: The firm only receives royalty fees rather than 100% of retail profits.

#### Example
**McDonald’s**, KFC, and Subway expanding their restaurant chains globally by partnering with local business owners who buy franchise rights.

---

### 8. Joint Venture

#### Meaning
Establishing a new business entity that is jointly owned and controlled by two or more independent companies (usually a foreign MNC and a host-country firm).

#### Advantages
* **Local Market Knowledge**: Local partners provide valuable knowledge of government rules, culture, and distribution.
* **Shared Costs and Risks**: Development costs and market risks are split between the partners.
* **Political Acceptability**: JVs are viewed favorably by host governments and bypass foreign ownership restrictions.

#### Disadvantages
* **Loss of Technology Control**: The MNC may leak critical technology and manufacturing secrets to the partner.
* **Management Conflicts**: Differences in corporate culture, management styles, and profit-reinvestment goals can lead to deadlock.
* **Lack of Strategic Flexibility**: Rigid JV agreements prevent quick strategic changes.

#### Example
**Starbucks** entering the Indian market through a 50/50 joint venture with the **Tata Group** (Tata Starbucks Private Limited).

---

### 9. Wholly Owned Subsidiary

#### Meaning
The parent company owns 100% of the stock of the foreign operations. This is achieved either by building a new facility from scratch (**Greenfield**) or by buying an existing local company (**Acquisition**).

#### Advantages
* **Complete Control over Technology**: Eliminates the risk of leaking trade secrets or IP.
* **Operational Coordination**: HQ can tightly coordinate marketing, pricing, and transfer pricing.
* **100% Profits**: No need to share revenues or profits with partners.

#### Disadvantages
* **Maximum Cost and Complexity**: The MNC must finance the entire setup, land, machinery, and hiring.
* **Maximum Risk**: The firm bears the full burden of host-country economic or political instability.
* **Cultural and Regulatory Barriers**: Learning host-country business practices without a partner takes time.

#### Example
Automotive companies like **Toyota** establishing wholly owned assembly and engine manufacturing plants in the United States and Europe.

---

### 10. Strategic Alliances

#### Meaning
Cooperative agreements between potential or actual competitors from different countries to collaborate on specific tasks (such as R&D or distribution) while remaining independent companies.

#### Advantages
* **Resource and Tech Sharing**: Partners pool expensive R&D costs and advanced technologies.
* **Risk Reduction**: Shared risks when developing next-generation products (e.g., autonomous driving).
* **Access to New Markets**: Leverages the local partner’s established sales and marketing network.
* **Speed**: Alliances can be formed quickly to capitalize on fast-moving global trends.

#### Disadvantages
* **Competitor Risk**: The partner may learn the firm's secrets and use them to compete.
* **Management Overhead**: Coordinating across two independent companies requires high communication and trust.

#### Example
Automobile companies partnering with tech firms to develop electric batteries or autonomous driving software.

---

### 11. Organization of International Business

#### Meaning
The **Organization of International Business** refers to the structural framework, control systems, and corporate culture that an MNC uses to coordinate its business activities across different countries. It establishes reporting lines, divides tasks, and ensures that regional offices and headquarters work toward the same corporate goals.

---

### 12. Importance of Organizational Structure

A well-designed organizational structure is essential because it:
1. **Improves Coordination and Communication**: Ensures smooth communication between HQ and global subsidiaries.
2. **Clarifies Roles and Responsibilities**: Eliminates confusion by defining who reports to whom.
3. **Supports Effective Decision-Making**: Speeds up decisions by outlining levels of authority.
4. **Helps Manage Global Operations Efficiently**: Handles the operational complexity of running factories and offices in different time zones.
5. **Improves Organizational Efficiency and Productivity**: Groups specialized teams by region or product to maximize output.

---

### 13. Types of Organizational Structures

MNCs select from seven core structural configurations depending on their size and strategy:
* **Functional Structure**: Grouped by business function (e.g., Marketing Head, Finance Head, HR Head).
* **Divisional Structure**: Grouped by product lines, service types, or geographic regions.
* **Matrix Structure**: Dual reporting lines. Employees report to both a functional manager and a divisional/regional manager.
* **Line Structure**: Simple, direct vertical chain of command from top to bottom.
* **Line & Staff Structure**: Line managers handle operations, supported by staff advisors (e.g., legal or technical experts).
* **Flat Structure**: Minimal hierarchical layers, giving lower-level employees high decision-making power.
* **Network Structure**: A small core company handles strategy and outsources manufacturing, logistics, and sales to partner firms.

---

### 14. Case Study: Starbucks' International Expansion Strategy

#### Background
When Starbucks decided to expand into the Indian market, it realized that the local coffee market was highly competitive and dominated by local tastes, tea preferences, and established cafes. Rather than entering independently, Starbucks chose a collaborative approach.

#### The Strategic Choice
In 2012, Starbucks formed a 50/50 joint venture called **Tata Starbucks Private Limited** with the **Tata Group** (specifically Tata Global Beverages). 

```
Starbucks (Global Brand & Coffee Expertise) ──► [ Joint Venture ] ◄── Tata Group (Local Supply Chain & Real Estate)
```

#### Why Tata was the Perfect Partner
* **Supply Chain**: Tata is one of the largest coffee growers in India, giving Starbucks access to premium local coffee beans.
* **Real Estate**: Tata owned prime commercial real estate, helping Starbucks open cafes in premium locations quickly.
* **Regulatory Compliance**: Tata’s deep political and regulatory experience helped Starbucks navigate India's bureaucratic licensing processes.

#### Outcome
Tata Starbucks expanded rapidly, opening hundreds of premium cafes across major Indian cities. The JV allowed Starbucks to adapt its menu (adding Indian-inspired food items like paneer wraps and local cardamom-spiced tea) while maintaining its premium global store aesthetic.

### 15. Global Pressures

Multinational corporations must balance two conflicting market forces:

```
                          GLOBAL PRESSURE MATRIX
        ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        ▼                                               ▼
Pressures for Cost Reduction              Pressures for Local Responsiveness
• Force: Standardize products             • Force: Adapt products
• Goal: Lower costs (economies of scale)  • Goal: Fit local tastes/rules
• Common in: Commodity goods (steel, chips)• Common in: Food, consumer retail
```

#### 1. Pressures for Cost Reduction
Firms face intense cost pressures in industries where:
* The product is a commodity (e.g., steel, oil, computer memory chips) where price is the primary competitive weapon.
* Competitors are based in low-cost manufacturing locations.
* Customers are powerful and face low switching costs.
* *Response:* MNCs must standardize products and manufacture in high-scale, low-cost central hubs.

#### 2. Pressures for Local Responsiveness
Firms face pressures to customize products and marketing in response to:
* **Differences in Consumer Tastes & Preferences**: (e.g., food ingredients, fashion styles).
* **Differences in Infrastructure & Traditional Practices**: (e.g., electrical voltages 110V vs 220V, driving on left vs right side).
* **Differences in Distribution Channels**: (e.g., selling through superstores in the US vs mom-and-pop shops in India).
* **Host Government Demands**: (e.g., local content requirements, medical device regulations).

---

### 16. The Integration-Responsiveness (GLIT) Matrix

By mapping these two pressures on a 2x2 grid, firms determine their global strategic path:

```
                  High │ 🟩 Global Standardization   🟪 Transnational
                       │ (e.g., Intel, Boeing)       (e.g., Caterpillar)
Pressure for           │
Cost Reduction         ├─────────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────────
                       │ 🟦 International            🟨 Localization
                   Low │ (e.g., Rolex, Xerox)        (e.g., McDonald's, Heinz)
                       └─────────────────────────────┴─────────────────────────────
                                     Low                         High
                                         Pressure for Local Responsiveness
```

#### A. International Strategy (Low Cost / Low Responsiveness)
Firms take products developed for their domestic market and sell them internationally with minimal local customization. R&D is kept at home.
* *Usage:* When the MNC has a unique product that local competitors lack.
* *Example:* **Rolex** (watches are identical worldwide).

#### B. Global Standardization Strategy (High Cost / Low Responsiveness)
Firms focus on achieving low-cost operations by standardizing products and marketing globally, utilizing location economies and economies of scale.
* *Usage:* When price competition is fierce but local differences are minimal.
* *Example:* **Intel** (microchips are identical worldwide).

#### C. Localization Strategy (Low Cost / High Responsiveness)
Firms customize their products, marketing, and operations to match national tastes. Each country subsidiary acts as a self-contained unit.
* *Usage:* When local tastes are highly distinct but price competition is moderate.
* *Example:* **Heinz** (adapting food recipes for different regions).

#### D. Transnational Strategy (High Cost / High Responsiveness)
Firms simultaneously try to achieve low-cost operations (through global standardization) and high local responsiveness (through local customization). Skills and technology flow multi-directionally among all subsidiaries.
* *Usage:* Highly complex; requires matrix configurations.
* *Example:* **Caterpillar** (standardizing engines, customizing attachment cabins).

### 17. Specialized Entry Modes

#### Turnkey Projects
A contract where a firm (the contractor) agrees to handle every detail of a project for a foreign client, including design, construction, and training of local operating personnel. At project completion, the key is handed over to the client.
* **Pros**: Highly lucrative for engineering firms; bypasses FDI restrictions in sensitive sectors (e.g., national energy grids or railways).
* **Cons**: No long-term interest in the foreign country; risk of creating a competitor by transferring advanced engineering skills.
* **Example**: Alstom building a high-speed metro line system for a foreign city and handing it over to the local government.

---

### 18. Strategic Alliance Management

To prevent strategic alliances from failing, firms must manage three distinct phases:

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        ▼                                                         ▼
Phase 1: Partner Selection   ──► Phase 2: Alliance Structure ──► Phase 3: Alliance Management
• Goal: Find partner with         • Goal: Write contract to       • Goal: Build trust and
  shared vision & resources         protect IP & trade secrets      foster learning
```

1. **Partner Selection**: A good partner must have resources the firm lacks, share its strategic goals, and not use the alliance to steal technology.
2. **Alliance Structure**: The agreement should protect critical IP. Techniques include:
   * Wall-off technology: Restrict certain technologies to the home country.
   * Contractual safeguards: Include strict clauses preventing unauthorized technology use.
   * Swap agreements: Agree to cross-license technologies so both parties gain equally.
3. **Managing the Alliance**: Build interpersonal relationships and trust ("social capital") between managers, along with learning from the partner to upgrade the firm's internal capabilities.

---

### 19. Vertical Differentiation: Centralization vs. Decentralization

#### Centralization (HQ Control)
* **Advantages**:
  * Ensures decisions align with corporate-wide objectives.
  * Avoids duplication of functional activities (e.g., R&D).
  * Facilitates coordinate action across global divisions.
* **Disadvantages**:
  * Bureaucratic delays; slow response to local market changes.
  * Lowers motivation for regional managers.

#### Decentralization (Subsidiary Autonomy)
* **Advantages**:
  * Fast decision-making in response to local market shifts.
  * Boosts manager motivation and initiative.
* **Disadvantages**:
  * Risk of poor coordination between subsidiaries.
  * Duplication of resources across countries.

---

### 20. MNC Horizontal Designs

Multinational firms organize their international operations into three main structures:

#### 1. Worldwide Area Structure
Foreign operations are organized into geographic divisions (e.g., Europe, Asia-Pacific, North America).
* *Alignment:* Best for **Localization Strategy**. Country managers have high autonomy.
* *Pros:* High local responsiveness.
* *Cons:* Prevents economies of scale; creates duplicate factories.

#### 2. Worldwide Product Division Structure
Foreign operations are organized around global product lines rather than geography.
* *Alignment:* Best for **Global Standardization Strategy**.
* *Pros:* Maximizes economies of scale; centralizes R&D.
* *Cons:* Weak local responsiveness.

#### 3. Global Matrix Structure
Dual reporting lines. A country manager reports to both a Geographic Area Head and a Product Division Head.
* *Alignment:* Best for **Transnational Strategy**.
* *Pros:* Balances cost pressures and local responsiveness.
* *Cons:* High conflict; slow decision-making due to dual reporting power struggles.

---

### 21. MNC Control Systems

To monitor global subsidiaries, MNCs use three control systems:
* **Output Controls**: Setting objective, quantifiable targets (e.g., sales goals, return on investment).
* **Bureaucratic Controls**: Relying on rules, standardized budgets, and operational manuals to direct employee behavior.
* **Cultural Controls**: Developing a strong corporate culture where employees internalize the company's values, reducing the need for direct supervision.

---

### 22. Solved Corporate Cases

#### Case 1: Tesla's Greenfield Strategy in Shanghai
* **Background**: Historically, China legally forced foreign automakers to enter via 50/50 joint ventures (e.g., GM partnering with SAIC). In 2018, China lifted this rule for electric vehicle manufacturers.
* **The Choice**: Tesla chose a **Greenfield Wholly Owned Subsidiary** to build "Gigafactory Shanghai".
* **Strategic Reasons**:
  * **IP Protection**: Tesla avoided leaking its battery management software and self-driving codes to Chinese partners.
  * **Subsidies**: The Shanghai government offered subsidized loans and land.
  * **100% Profits**: Tesla retained all sales revenues.
* **Analysis**: This case shows when a Greenfield entry mode is preferred: when protecting proprietary technology is critical and the host government offers favorable incentives.

#### Case 2: Lenovo's Acquisition of IBM’s PC Division
* **Background**: In 2005, Chinese PC manufacturer Lenovo acquired IBM’s personal computer business for $1.75 billion.
* **Strategic Reasons**:
  * **Speed to Market**: Lenovo instantly acquired IBM’s globally respected brand ("ThinkPad").
  * **Sales Channels**: Gained access to IBM's corporate sales networks in the US and Europe.
  * **Capability Synergy**: Combined Lenovo's low-cost assembly plants in China with IBM’s R&D capabilities.
* **Analysis**: This case shows when an Acquisition mode is preferred: when a firm needs to enter a highly competitive market quickly and acquire established brands, distribution networks, or technologies.
